Page Key
A guide to every color, label, symbol, and element used on this page
Pillar colors — used throughout all sections
Green — Power & Energy
Assigned to all power generation, UPS, distribution, and backup systems. Appears on pillar cards, timeline bars, company dots, and ETF headers.
Cyan — Cooling & Thermal
Assigned to all thermal management systems: air, liquid, immersion, and phase-change cooling. The page's primary accent color.
Gray — Physical Structure
Assigned to racks, cages, raised floors, and physical containment systems. Gray signals structural/inert elements.
Purple — Compute Substrate
Assigned to compute hardware across all eras: vacuum tubes, silicon CPUs, GPUs, ASICs, and future photonic/quantum processors.
Amber — Connectivity
Assigned to all interconnect technologies: copper, fiber, InfiniBand, silicon photonics, and future co-packaged optics.
Pink — Security & Access Control
Assigned to physical security, cybersecurity hardware, biometrics, HSMs, and surveillance systems.
Orange/Red — Storage
Assigned to all data persistence technologies: magnetic tape, HDD, NAND SSD, NVMe, and future DNA/holographic storage.
Teal — Monitoring & DCIM
Assigned to environmental sensors, DCIM software, building management systems (BMS), and AI-driven autonomous operations (AIOps).
Stability tier badges — shown on each pillar card
Eternal
Eternal
This pillar is required by every data center regardless of the compute technology inside — past, present, or future. Rooted in physics (thermodynamics, electromagnetism) or fundamental operational necessity. Will not be engineered away.
Evolving fast
Evolving fast
The need is eternal but the technology that fulfills it changes dramatically each generation. The substrate or medium transforms — silicon → photonic, HDD → DNA — while the underlying function (compute, remember) stays constant.
Emerging layer
Emerging layer
This pillar is becoming universal as complexity scales. Not always present in early data centers, but now mission-critical and growing in importance with every generation of infrastructure.
ETF badges — shown on each ETF card
ER 0.50%
ER — Expense Ratio
The annual fee charged by the ETF as a percentage of your investment. A 0.50% ER means you pay $5/year per $1,000 invested. Lower is better for long-term holders.
$11.7B
AUM — Assets Under Management
Total market value of all assets held by the ETF. Higher AUM generally means better liquidity, tighter bid-ask spreads, and lower risk of the fund closing.
Relevance dots — shown on pillar cards (bottom row)
5 filled dots — Maximum criticality
This pillar is the single most direct constraint on all data center operation. No workaround exists. Power and cooling both score 5 — remove either and the facility fails instantly.
4 filled dots — High criticality
Essential but slightly less immediate than 5-dot pillars. The facility can tolerate brief degradation, but long-term operation is impossible without it. Physical structure and security score 4.
3 filled dots — Important / growing
Critical at scale but relatively newer as a mandatory layer. Monitoring and DCIM score 3 — small facilities operated without formal DCIM for decades, but modern hyperscale operations cannot.
Empty dots — Not applicable
Unfilled dots represent unused criticality slots. The scale runs 1–5; no pillar scores below 3 on this page because all 8 were selected for being universally required.
Left accent bar — on active/expanded pillar cards
Colored left border
Appears when you click a pillar card to expand its detail panel. The bar color matches that pillar's assigned color from the pillar color key above, providing a visual anchor while reading the expanded content.
Timeline chart — component evolution 1960–2050+
Bar length = active era span
Each horizontal bar starts at the year the technology entered mainstream data center use and ends at the year it is expected to remain relevant (2060 = 2050+ on the axis label). Longer bar = longer-lived technology.
Bar color = pillar category
Timeline bar colors map directly to the pillar color system (green = power, cyan = cooling, gray = structure, etc.). Hover any bar to see the exact year range in a tooltip.
2050+
2050+ axis label
Any technology bar reaching the right edge of the chart (year 2060 internally) is labeled "2050+" to indicate it is not projected to be obsolete within the planning horizon of this analysis.

Company grid color dots

The dot color in the "Dominant Companies by Pillar" grid identifies which pillar that company primarily serves. Matches the pillar color system — green dot = power pillar, cyan = cooling, etc.
ETF coverage cards
SMH
Large colored ETF ticker
The ETF's exchange ticker symbol, colored to match its pillar. Clicking it opens a Google search for that ETF. Each ETF is grouped under the pillar it most directly provides investment exposure to.
Sector allocation bars
Horizontal bars show the ETF's approximate portfolio allocation across sectors. Bar length is proportional — the longest bar is always the largest sector allocation. Percentages are shown to the right of each bar.
NVDA
Top holding chips
Small labeled chips listing the ETF's largest individual stock positions. Percentages shown in parentheses where available. Each chip is clickable and links to a Google stock search for that holding.
Proxy
Proxy ETF notation
Some pillars (Cooling, Storage, Connectivity) have no dedicated pure-play ETF. In these cases the listed ETF is the best available proxy — a fund with significant but not exclusive exposure to that pillar's companies.
Typography & text styles

Syne — Display / headings
Used for all major section titles, pillar names, and the hero headline. Heavy weight (800) conveys structural authority. Chosen for its geometric, technical character.
Aa
DM Sans — Body text
Used for all descriptive text, pillar sub-labels, ETF focus descriptions, and explanatory copy. Optimized for readability at small sizes on dark backgrounds.

Used for all data-forward elements: ticker symbols, expense ratios, AUM figures, stat numbers, section counts, and badge text. Monospace ensures numerical alignment and a technical, data-dense aesthetic.

Small caps mono label
Section sub-labels and category dividers appear in uppercase Space Mono at 9–11px with wide letter-spacing. These act as organizational signposts without competing with content text.
Background depth layers
Page background (#0A0C0F)
The deepest layer. Near-black with a very slight cool blue tint. All content sits above this. Chosen to reduce eye strain for extended reading while maintaining strong contrast with text.
Card background (#111418)
Used for pillar cards, company cards, ETF cards, and the timeline chart wrapper. One step lighter than the page background, creating subtle depth without harsh contrast.
Expanded panel background (#181C22)
Used for the pillar detail panel that opens when you click a card, and for hover states. A third depth layer that signals "this content is nested inside the card above."
Active card background (#1E2330)
The currently selected/active pillar card shifts to this slightly blue-tinted dark layer to indicate selection, distinguishing it from all other cards in the grid.
Grid separator / gap color
The 2px gaps between cards in the pillar and company grids use this semi-transparent white. The grid background itself is this color, so the gaps appear as thin glowing lines between cards.
